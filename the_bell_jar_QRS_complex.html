The Bell Jar

THE MATRIOSHKA OF DAMOCLES

The bell jar looms over everyone’s heads. I imagine that everybody has one, akin to Hegel’s Anima Mundi, a single concept that envelops all individually, connecting humanity not through a shared vital force, but through a shared vulnerability to the void. This bell jar is the sword of Damocles, suspended by a damaged thread, awaiting its fall. But its lethality varies as its primary function is not to kill, but to numb the sight and sound of its victim, severing them from the frequency of the living.

When analyzing the descent into this state, we see it is not merely a philosophical crisis, but a literal, inescapable underworld. Esther’s reality mirrors this internal damnation perfectly: “I told him I believed in hell, and that certain people, like me, had to live in hell before they died.”

World of Darkness

We often make the mistake of viewing depression as a singular event, a single glass dome that drops over us. In reality, we have more than one bell jar above our heads, ready to fall one after the other, a matrioshka of jars. Some are transparent, some are grey, some are pitch black. Some are paper-thin, others agonizingly thick. This overlapping of barriers explains the chronic paralysis of the self.

As the jars multiply, the outside world becomes inaccessible, and even the abundance of choice becomes a form of starvation. This is the tragedy of the fig tree: “I saw myself sitting in the crotch of this fig-tree, starving to death, just because I couldn't make up my mind which of the figs I would choose.” The matrioshka effect ensures that even if one jar is lifted by the support of others, another remains firmly tethered to your shoulders.

I never quite saw others’ depression as mine. When they seemed to have a glimmer of hope, I felt suspended in a murky, tarry sea, while they appeared to be merely standing in a shadow or penumbra. Yet, looking through the one-way mirror of my own bell jar, I felt exposed, broken, defective, and undesirable. It is this exact distortion of the glass that turns internal suffering into a belief of fundamental, intrinsic failure, an echo of Esther’s own quiet realization: “The trouble was, I had been inadequate all along, I simply hadn't thought about it.”

QRS and Genetics of the Dead

Depression is not the spectacular, singular event of Icarus’ ascent and fall. You can always rise again, but like a sine wave, its amplitude can constantly fluctuate, it becomes the QRS interval of your life. It functions akin to a dormant virus, like HIV, laying low while medicated, viral load zero, yet always there, woven into the fabric of your being, ready to resurface.

When the mind is entirely consumed by the suffocating air of the bell jar, the body remains the sole, stubborn rebel. Even when the amplitude of life drops to its absolute lowest, the mechanical rhythm of the QRS persists.

“I took a deep breath and listened to the old brag of my heart. I am, I am, I am.”

This is not necessarily a triumphant roar of the liberated soul, but rather the obstinate machinery of survival. The heart brags not of happiness, but of mere, inescapable existence, forcing the victim to continue even when the mind yearns for the void.

This persistent survival inevitably forces a reemergence, but the illusion is that this return washes you clean. Esther longs for a systemic reset: “There ought, I thought, to be a ritual for being born twice – patched, retreaded and approved for the road.” So did I.

But being born twice, like a moth breaking from a chrysalis or a phoenix rising from the ash, apparently also means inheriting the genetics of the dead. Some silk will always cling to your newly formed wings; some embers will always burn your crimson feathers from reemergence onward. You cannot sanitize the trauma of the glass dome. As Esther realizes, despite her desperate desire for erasure: “Maybe forgetfulness, like a kind snow, should numb and cover them. But they were part of me.”

Therefore, the narrative concludes on a note of haunting, quiet uncertainty. Is she truly out? Is the bell jar shattered? It isn’t. At most, the thickness of the glass has been reduced, its transparency raised. The threat is never permanently eradicated, merely suspended in its descent: “All the heat and fear had purged itself. I felt surprisingly at peace. The bell jar hung, suspended, a few feet above my head.” The sword of Damocles hasn't vanished; it is simply waiting, resting in the penumbra, casting its lifelong shadow over the survivor.

Familial Gravity

Writing about the bell jar is an act of psychological tear. There’s a paradox built into chronic depression: on one hand, the mind is seduced by the gravity of familiar pain, yearning to retreat into the dark, predictable comfort of the glass dome. A terrifying safety in the void, much like the Kierkegaardian anatomy of despair, the self begins to stubbornly cling to its own torment because the illness becomes indistinguishable from identity itself. As I previously thought, it feels like a shame to shrug off such a durable coat, especially when black suits everyone. On the other hand, the survivor’s instinct fights desperately to suppress the paralyzing fear of ever returning to that position. To write about the jar, to analyze its thickness and its weight, is to stand precisely on the fracture line between the seductive pull of the familiar abyss and the fragile, exhausting resistance against it.

The Shadow and the Mirror

Isolation is rarely solitary within the ecosystem of the jar, and sometimes, the mind latches onto a physical manifestation of its own repressed despair, a Jungian Shadow. Joan serves as this dark mirror for Esther. She is the double, tracing Esther’s footsteps, dating the same man, arriving at the same impasses, ending up in the same psychiatric ward. The directional split happens in the absolute submission to the Void that Joan embodies and Esther is exhausting herself trying to fight.

Esther’s disdain for Joan is the ego’s rejection of its own shadow: “I like you. That’s tough, Joan. I said, picking up my book. Because I don’t like you. You make me puke, if you want to know.” Joan clings to Esther as a relentless reminder of the jar’s ultimate consequence, hovering “like a large and breathless fruit fly - as if the sweetness of recovery were something she could suck up.”

The shadow’s destruction is sometimes what allows the ego to survive. Joan’s eventual suicide functions as a proxy death. When the double succumbs entirely to the crushing weight of the bell jar, Esther is left standing, forced to listen to the mechanical persistence of her own body. Standing at Joan’s funeral, taking a deep breath to hear the old brag of her heart—“I am, I am, I am” is the frostbitten integration of the shadow. Esther survives, but she inherits the psychological embers of Joan’s demise. The bell jar may hang suspended, but the shadow has been permanently assimilated into the genetics of her own survival.

Katabasis

Solitude and alienation reach their zenith not when you are severed from the external world, but when you become disconnected from your own being. A fracture forms between the body and the mind. The mind realizes, that the body is merely a machine that does not possess the capacity to desire, but strictly executes. Depression places a halt on the mind's desire to be, yet the biological machine continues its function.

This mechanical persistence transforms existence into a pre-mortem katabasis. Esther articulates this descent when she states that certain people, like her, must live in hell before they die. But her descent into the modern inferno, navigating the circles of various psychiatric institutions and hospital wings, is entirely fruitless. Her journey yields no transcendental revelation, the only "truth" she brings back to the surface is the stark realization that she exists solely as a biological function. The thick glass of the bell jar deafens all philosophical reason to be, leaving only the mechanical heartbeat of the machine.

Kierkegaard’s Lake

Within the inferno, the flow of time is corrupted. Esther’s paralysis in front of the fig tree is not a simple moment of adolescent indecision regarding her career or love life, it is an extreme, Kierkegaardian despair intrinsically tied to the failure of constructing the self.

The bell jar completely annuls the future. It transforms time from a dynamic stream of opportunities into a murky, tarry lake of potential failures. To choose one fig is to actively enact the death of all the others, therefore, the mind chooses nothing, allowing the self to rot in the crotch of the tree alongside its unfulfilled potential. Navigating this despair leaves the victim numb to the concept of hope.

The Anchor

The struggle against the void is tethered to lucidity about existence. Sometimes, I feel like I am alive solely because I cannot bring myself to believe in anything other than this raw, immediate existence. The human being feels far too insignificant to warrant the grand architecture of an afterlife, and the concept of reincarnation is profoundly pointless.

If we are born twice, if the slate is wiped entirely clean and we forget the darkness, then all suffering and existence itself loses its meaning. I am my mind and I am my memory, after all. To wish for the absolute erasure of the pain, to hope the bell jar never existed, is to wish for the erasure of the self. We are built from the genetics of the dead, and the ashes of our past ascents and descents. Even if it is a muddy lake, and even if the glass remains suspended above, it is the memory of air that ultimately defines the shape of our lungs.
